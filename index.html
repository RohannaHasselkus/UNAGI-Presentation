An Introduction to Gene Regulatory Networks

  • A GRN is a map of how genes control the activity of eachother (de la Fuente, 2010).

  • Genes code for proteins such as transcription that repress or turn off other genes (Macneil & Walhout, 2011).

  • These interactions work on a larger scale to affect cell behavior (Hartmann et al., 2019).

Example Gene Regulatory Networks

    Bistable Toggle Switch: Two genes, A and B mutually inhibit each other. This creates bistability where the system is stable with only one of the genes on (Gardner et al., 2000).

    Repressilator: Gene A represses Gene B, Gene B represses Gene C and Gene C represses gene A. The delayed repression and feedback create oscillatory dynamics in gene expression. This means that the gene levels rise and fall in cycles. (Elowitz & Leibler, 2000)

    Signaling Cascade: A activates B, B activates C and C activates A. A small initial input produces a large output in gene expression downstream (Morrison, 2012).

The Importance of Gene Regulatory Networks

GRNs help explain how identical genomes can give rise to distinct cell types. By analyzing these networks, researchers can identify key genes that act as control points or “hubs” of regulation.

  • Disease insight: Disruption in regulatory networks contributes to diseases like cancer, diabetes, and developmental disorders.
  • Evolutionary insight: GRNs reveal how gene modules evolve and adapt to new environments.
  • Systems biology: GRNs bridge molecular biology and computational modeling, forming a foundation for predictive biology.

Unipartite and Bipartite Networks

In network theory, the structure of connections determines how information flows.

  • Unipartite networks contain nodes of a single "type" that can all affect eachother.
  • Examples using unipartite network applications: protein-protein interaction networks, gene co-expression networks, metabolic networks
  • Bipartite networks include two distinct node "types"
  • Examples of bipartite network applications: gene-disease associations, gene-TF association
  • Bipartite structures allow us to model the separation between regulators and their targets, crucial for interpreting transcriptional hierarchies.

Converting bipartite to unipartite projections allows comparison between regulatory and functional modules.

Background: BLOBFISH

Bipartite Limited Subnetworks from Multiple Observations using Breadth-First Search with Constrained Hops

  • Objective: Find compact subnetwork connecting the members of a pre-specified set of genes
  • Breadth-first search (Greedy algorithm) to go from a 'seed' set of nodes and create compact subnetwork
  • Creates a bipartite subnetwork → therefore, only works with even number hop networks
  • Computational inference (co-expression, Bayesian networks, etc.)

Seed Genes

Researchers can observe from literature which genes are associated with certain interactions. When studying certain associations, it is essential to be able to study from the standpoint of certain already known genest.

  • Definition: Seed genes are a preselected subset of genes chosen for their known involvement in a specific biological process, phenotype, or condition.
  • Purpose: They act as “anchors” or “entry points” to uncover related regulatory partners through network algorithms like BLOBFISH and UNAGI.
  • Importance:
    • Focuses computational search on biologically relevant regions of the network.
    • Improves interpretability by centering on genes with prior evidence or clinical significance.
    • Helps detect hidden or novel connections between known players.
  • Examples of use:
    • In cancer research, TP53 or BRCA1 may serve as seed genes to identify tumor-suppressor pathways.
    • In adipose tissue studies, PPARG or FABP4 can seed networks linked to lipid metabolism.
    • In muscle tissue, MYOD1 and ACTN2 can initiate subnetworks governing differentiation and contractility.
    • In developmental biology, Hox genes often serve as seeds to explore spatial patterning modules.

Introduction: UNAGI

Unipartite Network Adaptation using Gene Influence

  • Unipartite adaption of blobfish (able to jump across all hop counts and computes for all under given content eg. Hop=3 compute 1,2 and 3 hops)
  • Uses all the same elements as BLOBFISH: Breadth-first search algorithm, objective to find compact subnetwork

Theorem 1

Theorem 1. Any path Pb,c between nodes b and c in G contains a node a such that

Da,b = Da,c,

where Di,j represents the shortest unweighted graph distance between two nodes i and j.

Since b and c are of the same node type in a bipartite network, Pb,c must contain an odd number of intermediate nodes and an even number of connecting edges:

mod(Db,c, 2) = 0.

Moreover, Db,c = Dc,b.

Traversing the first Db,c/2 edges in Pb,c starting at b gives a path Pb,x where x ∉ {a,b}. Traversing backwards along the last Db,c/2 edges starting at c gives a path Pc,y where y ∉ {a,b}.

Thus,

Db,x = Dc,y = Db,c/2.

If x = y, then x satisfies the condition for node a. Assume for contradiction that x ≠ y.

Then either x is not connected to y or Dx,y > 0. Since Pb,c contains both x and y, some edges are missing, contradicting that it is a path.

If Dx,y > 0, then

Db,c = Db,x + Dx,y + Dc,y > Db,x + Dc,y.

Substituting Db,x = Dc,y = Db,c/2 yields

Db,c > Db,c/2 + Db,c/2,

contradicting Db,c = Db,c.

Hence x = y, so x = a, proving that Da,b = Da,c.

Theorem 2

Theorem 2. For any path Pb,c of length n between nodes b and c in G, and any integers nb, nc ≥ 0 such that nb + nc = n, there exists a unique node a lying in Pb,c such that

Da,b = nb    and    Da,c = nc,

where Di,j represents the shortest unweighted graph distance between two nodes i and j.

Case 1: nb = 0. Then nc = n. Axiomatically, Db,b = 0 = nb. Further, Db,c = n = nc. Therefore, b satisfies the condition for node a.

Case 2: nc = 0. Then nb = n. Axiomatically, Dc,c = 0 = nc. Further, Dc,b = n = nb. Therefore, c satisfies the condition for node a.

Case 3: nb, nc > 0. We have Db,c = Dc,b.

Traversing the first nb edges in Pb,c starting at b results in a path Pb,x where x ∉ {a,b}. Traversing backwards along the last nc edges starting at c results in a path Pc,y where y ∉ {a,b}.

Therefore,

Db,x = nb    and    Dc,y = nc.

If x = y, then x satisfies the condition for node a.

Assume for contradiction that x ≠ y. Then either x is not connected to y or Dx,y > 0. Since Pb,c contains both x and y, not all edges in Pb,c are connected, contradicting its definition as a path.

If Dx,y > 0, then

Db,c = Db,x + Dx,y + Dc,y > Db,x + Dc,y.

By definition of Pb,c, Db,c = n. Substituting Db,x = nb and Dc,y = nc gives

n > nb + nc = n,

a contradiction.

Thus x = y, so x = a, proving that Da,b = nb and Da,c = nc.

Validation

  • BLOBFISH used the following tissues to validate the algorithm: Musculoskeletal, Artery, Skin, Lung, and Adipose.
  • We replicate this with UNAGI, initially creating GGMs (Gaussian Graphical Models), which represent conditional dependencies between genes, and then generating Gene Correlation Networks, which connect genes based on pairwise correlation in expression.
  • Filtered the top and bottom 2.5% of the networks.
  • Intentional use of varied tissues with significantly different functions to clearly validate algorithm functionality.
  • Selected two tissue types: Adipogenic and Skeletal-muscular.
  • Found the genes associated with these tissues, while treating other tissues as "noise."
  • Verification step: genes associated with these tissues should not appear as "associated" with other tissues.

Overview of Tissues

Musculoskeletal Tissue

  • Responsible for conscious movements of the body
  • Also known as striated muscle due to fine red and white line patterns
  • Muscle fibers (fasciculi) are surrounded by endomysium, bundled by perimysium, and enclosed in epimysium
  • Fibers are made of myofibrils → contain multiple myofilaments
  • Striated arrangement forms sarcomeres, the primary contracting unit of the muscle

Adipose Tissue

  • Two types: white adipose tissue (WAT) and brown adipose tissue (BAT) — WAT was analyzed
  • Functions: energy storage, endocrine signaling, and insulin sensitivity
  • Constructed from adipocytes (fat cells)
  • Cells contain a large single lipid droplet, with organelles on the periphery

Skin Tissue

  • Made of epidermis, dermis, and hypodermis; the dermis is the largest layer and primary collection site
  • Contains connective tissue, blood/lymphatic vessels, and hair follicles
  • Dermatomes: regions of skin linked to spinal nerves, transmit pain stimuli

Artery Tissue

  • Three layers:
    1. Tunica intima – inner layer with endothelial cells, enables oxygen-rich blood flow
    2. Media – contains smooth muscle that controls vasodilation and vasoconstriction
    3. Adventitia – outer layer connecting arteries to other body tissues

Lung Tissue

  • Respiratory epithelium includes: Ciliated, Goblet, Basal, Brush, and Neuroendocrine cells
  • Most abundant: Ciliated cells – regulate the mucociliary escalator
  • This escalator removes debris from the respiratory system

Results and Interpretation

UNAGI was tested across multiple tissues using genes of interest (GOIs) associated with adipose and musculoskeletal systems. The model successfully recovered these genes in their correct tissues, showing strong biological specificity and consistency with expected patterns.

Tissue GOIs Found Total GOIs Recovery Rate Notes
Adipose 7 7 100% All adipogenic genes correctly detected (PPARG, FABP4, ADIPOQ, etc.)
Skeletal 26 28 92.9% Strong recovery of myogenic genes (MYH2, MYL1, TTN, TPM1, etc.)
Skin 33 35 94.3% Overlap with muscle-associated genes due to shared cytoskeletal proteins
Lung 33 35 94.3% Expected background connectivity; reflects metabolic housekeeping genes
Artery 33 35 94.3% Consistent with structural and contractile gene overlap (e.g., smooth muscle)

Summary: UNAGI successfully distinguished tissue-specific gene modules, accurately recovering all adipose genes and nearly all skeletal-muscular genes. The slight overlap with non-target tissues (Skin, Lung, Artery) is likely due to shared structural or metabolic components.
